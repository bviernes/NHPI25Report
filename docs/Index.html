Native Hawaiian and Pacific Islander Veterans Health Status Report 2024

Cross-sectional Comparison of NHPI Veterans vs. Non-NHPI Veterans who received VA Care in 2024

Author

The Center for Pacific Islander Veteran Health

Published

June 23, 2025

1 CPIVH

2 Introduction

Native Hawaiian and Pacific Islander (NHPI) populations experience significant health disparities, particularly related to chronic diseases such as diabetes, hypertension, cardiovascular diseases, and obesity (Mau et al. 2009; Look et al. 2013). Despite documented disparities in the general NHPI population, research on NHPI groups often suffers from limited sample sizes, restricting the generalizability and robustness of findings (Kaholokula et al. 2020). Within the US Department of Veterans Affairs (VA) healthcare system, NHPI Veterans constitute a substantial group exceeding 100,000 individuals. However, the prevalence, severity, and management outcomes of chronic diseases among NHPI Veterans remain understudied (Subica et al. 2019). Understanding these patterns can help inform targeted interventions to improve clinical outcomes and reduce health disparities.

3 Objectives

This report aims to:

  1. Characterize the prevalence and severity of chronic diseases (diabetes, chronic kidney disease, hypertension, cardiovascular disease, obesity, gout, cancer, and chronic neurological disease) and those specifically relevant to Veteran populations (alzheimer’s and dementia, TBI/CTE, PTSD, cognitive impairment, substance abuse, and visual impairment) among NHPI Veterans.
  2. Compare disease condition prevalence and severity between NHPI Veterans and Non-NHPI Veterans as well as among NHPI subgroups.

4 Methods

  • Study Design and Population

Retrospective cross-sectional analysis using a dataset derived from the VA corporate data warehouse (CDW), including EHR and community care claims data. NHPI Veterans were identified using VA race/ethnicity indicators and NLP-extracted data created by the VA’s Center for Pacific Islander Veteran Health (CPIVH) (Viernes et al. 2025).

  • Data Sources

Variables include demographics (age, sex, geographic region), chronic disease indicators (diabetes, chronic kidney disease (CKD), hypertension, cardiovascular disease (CVD), obesity), and VA utilization metrics (observation time in VA, outpatient visit count, inpatient visit count, number of unique diagnoses recorded, number of prescriptions issued, and number of procedure codes recorded).

5 Methods cont.

  • Statistical Analysis

Analyses include descriptive statistics of conditions across groups using chi-squared and paired t-tests for statistical significance. Additionally, adjusted logistic regression analyses were performed to identify odds of having a specific disease given NHPI or Non-NHPI status.

  • Limitations/Caveats

Limited causal relationships can be developed from a cross-sectional analysis. Further the NHPI population used in this analysis is required to have multiple data points that confirm NHPI status. The Non-NHPI population is not, and maybe be biased due to the fact that multiple data points also is correlated with increased utilization and a more accurate health record. Generalizability is limited to Veterans receiving VA care.

19 Results

  • NHPI Veterans are on average, younger, less male, disproportionately high disability, and more likely to reside in the Pacific and Western US region.

  • NHPI veterans are shown to be at higher risk of many conditions when compared to non-NHPI veterans.

  • NHPI have higher utilization of VA care than non-NHPI, but…

  • NHPI much lower utilization of VA care when they live in the Pacific.

  • NHPI in the Pacific also have lower or similar recorded rates of many conditions, but higher rates of Diabetes and Gout.

20 Conclusions

These findings are steps forward in the area of NHPI-specific veteran research, but they also just highlight how much there still is to do. This data shows there are hundreds of more focused disease-specific analyses that could shed more light on these disparities.
